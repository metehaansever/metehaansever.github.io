Research

I study the microbial world using multi-omics methods, with particular experience in soil microbial ecology/biogeochemistry and the development of widely applicable proteomics software.

Microbial Activity in Arctic Soils

The motivation for my doctoral research was the question of how greater plant growth associated with Arctic warming affects soil microbial processes underlying the cycling of vast quantities of organic carbon. Metaproteomics -- the characterization of proteins in a sample that are expressed by multiple organisms -- can be used to understand myriad organic matter transformations that are intractable to direct measurement and the taxa responsible for these processes. I developed new software tools to enable the application of metaproteomics to complex samples such as soil.

The metaproteomic analysis of soils that I collected from Toolik Field Station in Arctic Alaska yielded significant insights into the functional differentiation of major bacterial groups and the variation of these taxa and their metabolic activity with vegetation. Protein expression among most proteobacterial groups is structured around the acquisition of small solutes and nitrogen, the limiting nutrient in tundra soils. Acidobacteria, a poorly understood but ubiquitous clade, is the most metabolically active group, and specializes in the degradation of relatively labile polysaccharides such as hemicelluloses and starch. Other groups invest in enzymes targeting other polymers -- Actinobacteria depolymerizes cellulose, Burkholderiaceae breaks down lignin, and Bacteroidetes and Myxococcales decompose pectin. Each taxonomic group maintains a characteristic protein expression profile between vegetation types. Proteobacterial functions, however, are more prevalent in greener floras favored in a warmer Arctic.

These results indicate a fundamental divide between proteobacterial taxa adapted to plant interactions in rhizosphere microenvironments and largely non-proteobacterial taxa adapted to the bulk soil. Proteobacterial groups dominate the expression of transporters for monosaccharides and sugar alcohols, which are likely root exudates, as these Proteobacteria participate minimally in polysaccharide decomposition. Carbon monoxide, which also appears to be a primarily proteobacterial substrate, is known to be produced mainly by roots in soils. Although all bacterial groups express proteins for intracellular nitrogen cycling at levels consistent with overall activity (extrapolated from ribosomal protein expression), proteobacterial groups dominate the production of nitrogen transporters, which suggests stiff competition with plants for scarce nitrogen around roots. Phosphorus transporters are much more evenly expressed across taxa, as expected from a more even concentration of phosphorus across soil microenvironments. Proteobacterial groups also appear to be the major producers of carbon storage polymers (polyhydroxyalkanoates), which are often associated with nutrient starvation. Stronger microbial nitrogen limitation in the rhizosphere than bulk soil supports the recent theory that microbial competition with plants rather than slow microbial organic matter degradation limits plant nitrogen uptake and therefore growth in the Arctic.

Regarding the comparison of cold- and warm-adapted plant ecotypes, the consistency of protein expression profiles suggests that the functional specialization of microbial taxa does not change with rapid Arctic greening, which often occurs in response to relatively small increases in temperature -- especially small below the surface in soils. This is encouraging for the prediction of biogeochemical trends from experimental manipulations of Arctic soils.

Software Development

I have developed Python tools for the analysis of peptide mass spectra and metaproteomic data. New computational approaches were essential for interpreting complex soil datasets and should prove useful in a variety of environmental and biomedical samples. The major challenge I encountered was the lack of an appropriate sequence database for identification of peptide mass spectra by the standard database search method. For many types of simple proteomic samples, the database is the reference genome of the sampled organism. The high genomic microdiversity of soil microbes limits the utility of database search against even paired metagenomic datasets, as a single amino acid mutation can significantly alter the mass properties of a peptide. The constraint of an appropriate reference database creates challenges for a variety of samples, not just environmental metaproteomes -- monoclonal antibodies, unexpected sequence variants, nonribosomal and post-translationally modified peptides, and proteins from fossils.

De novo sequencing is an alternative method to database search that attempts to directly solve the sequence of a peptide mass spectrum. I developed a post-processing machine learning tool, Postnovo, to improve the low accuracy of de novo sequences from existing programs. Postnovo is analogous in purpose to common database search post-processing tools such as Percolator and PeptideProphet. Largely through the development of new metrics and the identification of consensus sequences using a new and efficient dynamic programming algorithm, Postnovo increases the yield of accurate de novo sequences at a given FDR by about an order of magnitude. Further, it reliably estimates the posterior error probability of de novo sequences, allowing FDR control. See Postnovo's GitHub page for details.

I developed another tool, ProteinExpress, to better analyze metaproteomic data using metagenomic/transcriptomic reference datasets. ProteinExpress is designed to address the problem of database incompleteness in peptide-spectrum matching and protein inference. A match between a peptide mass spectrum and a protein-coding sequence does not guarantee that the mass spectrum originated from the taxon associated with the protein, as opposed to another taxon producing the same peptide in an unsampled coding sequence. ProteinExpress addresses the problem of taxonomic assignment by using a probabilistic metric for protein expression based on sequence similarity to taxonomic bins of contigs. ProteinExpress also increases the peptide identification rate by allowing the combination of multiple reference metagenomes/transcriptomes. The consideration of a suite of reference datasets from across Alaska was especially useful in my Arctic soil analyses, as cold soils have lower genomic microdiversity than warm soils. As ProteinExpress performs protein functional annotation using eggNOG-mapper, I also developed a curated database of broader Functional Groups based on eggNOG+Gene Family terms to ease human interpretability (e.g., Cellulases, Polyamine Synthesis, Glycolysis).

Teaching

I have been a teaching assistant in numerous courses at the University of Chicago, including my largest responsibility of designing and teaching labs in Environmental Chemistry.

I have developed new college environmental chemistry labs which I am preparing for publication in the Journal of Chemical Education. One involves measuring mercury levels in fish by atomic absorption spectroscopy and modeling blood mercury from the data. The other involves benchtop microcosms of the bioremediation of a persistant organic pollutant and the application of the rate measurements to a model of industrial wastewater treatment under a cost constraint.
